# Employee Survey: The Complete HR Guide

An employee survey is a structured method for gathering feedback from your workforce about their experiences, attitudes, and perceptions of the organisation. When designed and executed well, surveys are the most scalable listening tool available to HR.

This guide covers everything: types of surveys, how to design them, how to run them, and — most importantly — how to act on what you learn.

## Types of Employee Surveys

### Annual Engagement Survey
A comprehensive, organisation-wide survey run once or twice a year. Typically 40–60 questions covering all dimensions of engagement. Useful for strategic benchmarking but slow to detect emerging issues.

### Pulse Survey
Short (2–15 questions), frequent (monthly or quarterly), and fast. Pulse surveys are designed to track sentiment in near-real-time. They catch emerging problems early and keep the feedback loop active between annual surveys.

### Onboarding Survey
Triggered at specific milestones in a new employee's journey (day 7, 30, 60, 90). Captures first impressions, identifies integration friction, and flags manager issues early.

### Exit Survey
Conducted when an employee leaves. Often underutilised — or run too late and too informally to yield actionable data.

### Lifecycle Survey
Automated surveys tied to tenure milestones (1 year, 3 years, 5 years). Captures how employee experience evolves over time.

### eNPS Survey
A single question: "On a scale of 0–10, how likely are you to recommend this company as a place to work?" Simple, comparable, and a leading indicator of retention risk.

## Key Survey Design Principles

**1. Start with the decision you want to make.** Don't survey for general data — survey to answer a specific question that will change how you act.

**2. Keep it short.** Every question you add reduces completion rates. The ideal pulse survey is 3–8 questions.

**3. Mix question types.** Rating scales (1–5), multiple choice, and open-ended questions each capture different kinds of insight. Open-ended questions with sentiment analysis reveal what scales miss.

**4. Guarantee anonymity.** Not just in policy — in the platform architecture and visibly in the survey interface.

**5. Close the loop.** Share results. Announce actions. Run a follow-up survey to verify change.

## Turning Results Into Action

The most common survey failure isn't bad design — it's the gap between collecting data and doing something about it.

A practical action planning workflow:
1. Review heatmap to identify critical departments and categories (score < 60)
2. Prioritise top 2–3 issues based on score and department size
3. Assign an owner to each action item
4. Set a 30/60/90 day milestone
5. Share actions with employees — specifically the people who flagged the issue
6. Re-survey on the same topic in the next cycle to measure change
