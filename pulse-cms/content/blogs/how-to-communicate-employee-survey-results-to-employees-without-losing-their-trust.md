# How to Communicate Survey Results Without Losing Employee Trust

The survey is done. The data is in. Now what?

This is where most organisations get it wrong. They either share nothing (because the results are uncomfortable), share a sanitised summary that employees see through immediately, or share raw data without context that triggers anxiety.

All three approaches damage trust. Here's how to do it right.

## The Non-Negotiable: You Must Share Results

Before anything else — commit to sharing something. Employees who give feedback and hear nothing in return are less engaged than employees who were never surveyed at all. Silence after a survey is the worst possible message.

## The Communication Framework

### Step 1: Acknowledge What You Heard (Within 2 Weeks)
Don't wait until you have solutions. Send a brief communication — email, Slack, all-hands — that says: "We've received and reviewed your responses. Here's what we heard at a high level, and here's what happens next."

### Step 2: Be Honest About the Hard Findings
Share the difficult data. If your Compensation & Benefits score is 21 and trending down, say so. Employees already know this — they experience it. Pretending the scores are better than they are is immediately credible damage.

### Step 3: Separate Facts from Interpretations
Share the score. Then explain what you think it means. Invite discussion. "Our Q1 engagement score in the Product team is 52. We think this is connected to the recent restructure and the lack of clarity on roadmap direction. We want to hear if you agree."

### Step 4: Announce Specific, Measurable Actions
Not "we're working on it." Specific: "We're launching bi-weekly roadmap briefings for the Product team starting April 1st, and we'll resurvey on alignment in June to see if it moves the needle."

### Step 5: Close the Loop in the Next Survey
The next survey cycle should explicitly ask: "Did you see the changes we made based on your last round of feedback?" This makes the feedback-to-action cycle visible and tangible.
