# B2B Deck Frameworks

Reference material for `sales-deck-generator`. Four frameworks (three narrative structures + three deck archetypes), slide count guidance, and speaker notes conventions.

---

## Framework 1: Andy Raskin's "Greatest Sales Deck" (5 parts)

Raskin's framework (popularized via his teardown of Zuora's deck) is the most battle-tested B2B pitch structure. Use it for **first-call pitch decks** and vision decks.

### The 5 parts

1. **Name a big, undeniable shift in the world.** Start with something the audience can't argue with. Not "here's our product" — "here's how the world just changed." (e.g., "73% of B2B pipeline is fake — AI has made it obvious.")

2. **Show there will be winners and losers.** Make the stakes personal. Companies that adapt to the shift will win; those that don't will lose. Don't be subtle — the audience should feel the urgency.

3. **Tease the Promised Land.** Paint the picture of what winning looks like after the shift. Specific, concrete, emotionally resonant. Not "you'll be successful" — "your CRO will stop asking if you'll hit the number because the forecast is finally real."

4. **Introduce the obstacles — and your magic.** Show what stands in the way of the Promised Land, then introduce your product as the unique way to overcome those obstacles. Note: the product shows up here, not in Part 1. This is the structural move that makes the framework work.

5. **Present the proof.** Customers, metrics, logos, quotes. Evidence that the Promised Land is real and your magic delivers.

### Slide-by-slide skeleton (12-slide version)

| # | Role | Example H1 |
|---|------|------------|
| 1 | Title | "<Brand> — <one-line positioning>" |
| 2 | The Shift | "The shift: <undeniable change>" |
| 3 | Why Now | "Why this is happening now" |
| 4 | Winners and Losers | "Winners do X. Losers do Y." |
| 5 | The Promised Land | "What winning looks like" |
| 6 | The Obstacle | "Why it's hard to get there" |
| 7 | Our Magic | "Introducing <Brand>" |
| 8 | How It Works | "The <N>-step approach" |
| 9 | Proof — Customers | "Customers who made the shift" |
| 10 | Proof — Outcomes | "Results" |
| 11 | Pricing / Path Forward | "How we work together" |
| 12 | Closing CTA | "Next step: <CTA>" |

## Framework 2: Problem-Agitate-Solve (with Proof Anchor)

Use this for **demo follow-up decks** and shorter pitches where you don't have time to build a full shift narrative.

### Structure

1. **Problem** — name the specific pain the audience feels today (concrete, bounded)
2. **Agitate** — make it worse. Show the cost of not solving (quantified where possible)
3. **Solve** — introduce the product as the answer (specific, demonstrable)
4. **Proof** — anchor with evidence (customer, metric, quote, screenshot)
5. **Next step** — explicit CTA

### Slide-by-slide skeleton (10-slide version)

| # | Role | Example H1 |
|---|------|------------|
| 1 | Title | Brand + positioning |
| 2 | The Problem | "Your reps spend 40% of their week on CRM theatre" |
| 3 | The Cost | "What pipeline fiction costs you" |
| 4 | The Solve | "Here's how <Brand> fixes it" |
| 5 | How It Works (1) | Core capability 1 |
| 6 | How It Works (2) | Core capability 2 |
| 7 | How It Works (3) | Core capability 3 |
| 8 | Proof | Customer story |
| 9 | Outcomes | Quantified results |
| 10 | CTA | "Next step" |

## Framework 3: Challenger Sale (Insight-Led)

Use this for **board / exec briefs** and sophisticated sales cycles where the buyer already knows the space.

### Structure

1. **Commercial insight** — a data-backed perspective on the business the buyer didn't have
2. **Reframe the problem** — show that the problem is different (or bigger) than they thought
3. **Emotional impact** — make the implication personal to the buyer's role
4. **New way** — present a better approach (not necessarily product-first)
5. **Your solution** — position the product as the execution of the new way
6. **Proof and path forward** — validate and commit

### Slide-by-slide skeleton (15-slide version)

| # | Role | Example H1 |
|---|------|------------|
| 1 | Title | "A new perspective on <category>" |
| 2-3 | Commercial Insight | Data-backed surprising claim + supporting chart |
| 4-5 | Reframe | "What you think the problem is" vs. "What it actually is" |
| 6 | Emotional Impact | "What this means for your role" |
| 7 | New Way | "The better approach" |
| 8-10 | Our Solution | Product capabilities tied to the new way |
| 11-12 | Proof | Customers + outcomes |
| 13 | Implementation | How rollout works |
| 14 | Investment | Pricing or commercial terms |
| 15 | Path Forward | Decision + timeline |

## Deck Archetypes

### Archetype A: Vision Deck
First-call pitch. 12 slides. Raskin framework. Heavy narrative, medium product depth, strong proof close.

### Archetype B: Demo Follow-Up Deck
Post-demo leave-behind. 10 slides. Problem-Agitate-Solve. Mirrors what was shown, anchors the ROI case, extends to a clear next step.

### Archetype C: Executive Brief
Sponsor-supporting deck for an internal champion. 15 slides. Challenger Sale. Dense on insight and reframe, lighter on product features, heavy on implementation clarity.

---

## Slide count guidance

| Archetype | Recommended count | Maximum |
|---|---|---|
| Vision deck | 12 | 15 |
| Demo follow-up | 10 | 12 |
| Executive brief | 15 | 20 |
| Board deck | 20 | 25 |

Shorter is almost always better. If the user requests 20+ slides for a pitch, push back and ask which slides they'd cut.

## Speaker notes conventions

**Length:** 2-3 sentences per slide. Notes should fit in the speaker-notes pane without scrolling.

**Content:**
- What to emphasize on the slide
- What to skip or gloss over
- The transition to the next slide

**Avoid:**
- Reading the slide verbatim
- Prescribing tone ("say this slowly") — presenters develop their own rhythm
- Essays or bullet lists longer than 3 lines

**Example:**

> **Slide 4 — Winners and Losers**
>
> Emphasize that the shift is already underway — don't let the audience treat it as theoretical. Name 1-2 companies (or their competitors, if you can) who are winning. Transition: "Here's what winning looks like in concrete terms."
